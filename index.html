Introduction

-scope |skəʊp|
combining form
denoting an instrument for observing, viewing, or examining: microscope | telescope .

Artscope is a augmented reality interface prototype for museums.
The interface is crafted to show more about the artwork standing in front of you without distracting you or interfering with your behavior.
Artscope recognizes and tracks the piece in front of you and it connects you with the invisible layers of information available.

Artscope was coded from the ground up in 24 hours during the HACK4DK2014 hackaton.

Artscope is a prototype, read more about this at the bottom of the page.

Invisible layers

Invisible layers reveal nothing new about the artwork. All the informations are already there, but are invisible to our eye. Analytical tools such as Xray, IR and UV reveal the history of the piece. Image analysis, on the other hand, reveal more about colors, their distribution and dominance.

X-rays

Under X-ray illumination, damage or alterations to paintings are suddenly revealed despite being invisible to the naked eye. For example, X-rays show wormholes in a painting. Not only this detail but also the broader history of a piece is revealed: the way any change is made reveals when modifications happened and vice versa.
A through interpretation of what X-rays can reveal needs to be performed by an heritage conservator.
Artscope can provide insight to conservators but also just stimulate the curiosity of those just observing the piece.

UV light

When UV light shines on a painting, one gets a fluorescence signal back from the materials used in the painting. Different types of varnish and pigments fluoresce with different shades. For example an old varnish has a stronger greenish fluorescence than a newer one. Conservators can also get information about whether repairs have been done around the same time that the piece was originally painted. If damage has been rectified long after the painting was created, materials that age differently than the originals fluoresce with a different color.

Infrared light

Infrared lights acts like your eyesight, on steroids. Infrared rays penetrate below the varnish layer and reveal the underlying drawings and sketches. Using infrared light conservators can search for a painting signature that can help to identify who created the painting. Infrared light pictures also show whether there have been changes in the original drawing along the years, thus revealing fun little details that are otherwise hidden from the viewer.

Interactive

Artscope aligns the visible with the invisible, allowing you to play and compare the different layers. It also works like you, the closer you go to the artwork the more details you see.

AstScope follows you, not vice versa. As soon as you step back and start to turn away from the piece similar artworks are presented for you to check out next, helping you to experience your personal path in the museum collection.

The power of data

Ever wanted to find an artwork created in the same region, or with the same technique, but in a different time? Artscope knows what artworks are available at the museum and will show you the ten most similar artworks. By showing the similarly broken down by its components, Artscope allows you to explore the museum following to your own path.

Similarity is learned with a machine learning algorithm that takes into account the semantic similarity between mediums and techniques, the time passed between the creation of two artworks and whether or not two artworks were created in the same country.

As much as we would love to say: go and get it on the app store, we can't. Artscope is strictly a prototype. The code base is full of dirty hacks, and it works just for one artworks and with the API that was set-up ad-hoc by SMK during the hackaton.

Artscope was an exploration of what's possible in hacking our cultural heritage in a week end.

Since you made it so far, you deserve more details. The application for iOS is made with Unity, whereas all the data processing and image analysis is done in python. The data analysis is done beforehand and the results are hardcoded into the Unity application. It's not unreasonable to scale this set-up using a client-server approach. Artwork recognition and tracking is set-up just for one artwork, but can be scaled up to the entire collection of the museum.
